🔍

The CRAAP Test

A framework for evaluating the quality of any information source

C — Currency

How recent is the information? Generally within 5 years for exercise science. Stable scientific laws may be older. Check the publication or revision date.

R — Relevance

Does it relate to your topic? Is it appropriate for your purpose and level? Is it the right type of source for your need?

A — Authority

Who wrote it? Are they qualified? Affiliated with a peak body, government organisation, or university? Check credentials and affiliation.

A — Accuracy

Is it evidence-based? Does it cite credible sources? Can you verify the information in at least two other credible sources?

P — Purpose

Why does this information exist? To inform, educate, sell, entertain, or persuade? Identify any bias or conflicts of interest. Ask: who benefits from you believing this?

⚠️

Red flags: No author listed · No date · .com with something to sell · No references · Extreme claims · "As seen on TV"

Cardiovascular System

The heart, blood vessels, and how blood circulates

Pulmonary (blue = deoxygenated) and systemic (red = oxygenated) circulation

Key Measurements

🫀 Resting HR: 60–80 bpm
📊 Blood Pressure: 120/80 mmHg
💧 Stroke Volume (SV): Volume per beat — plateaus at ~50% max HR
📈 Cardiac Output: HR × SV

Blood Vessels

🔴 Arteries — carry blood away from heart (oxygenated, except pulmonary artery)
🔵 Veins — return blood to heart (deoxygenated, except pulmonary veins)
🔗 Capillaries — site of O₂/CO₂ and nutrient exchange

Respiratory System

Breathing mechanics and gas exchange

📌

Path of air: Nose/Mouth → Pharynx → Larynx → Trachea → Bronchi → Bronchioles → Alveoli (gas exchange happens here — O₂ in, CO₂ out)

Breathing Mechanism

⬇️ Inhale: Diaphragm contracts (moves down), intercostals lift rib cage → chest expands → air rushes in

⬆️ Exhale: Mostly passive — diaphragm relaxes, rib cage drops → air pushed out

Key Numbers

💨 Normal resting rate: 12–16 breaths/min

🫁 Tidal volume: air per normal breath
📦 Total lung capacity: max air held
💪 Vital capacity: max exhale after max inhale
🔒 Residual volume: air always remaining

Muscle Function & Contraction Types

How muscles produce and control movement

Contraction Types

🏋️ Concentric: Muscle shortens under load. E.g. lifting phase of bicep curl

⬇️ Eccentric: Muscle lengthens under load. E.g. lowering phase. Causes DOMS

🧱 Isometric: No change in length. E.g. plank, wall sit

4 Muscle Roles

🎯 Prime mover (agonist): Causes the movement
🔄 Antagonist: Opposes movement, returns limb
🤝 Synergist: Assists prime mover
🏛️ Fixator/Stabiliser: Holds rest of body in place

Cartilage, Tendons & Ligaments

Connective structures of the musculoskeletal system

Tendon

A band of tough connective tissue that connects muscle to bone. Transmits the force of muscle contraction to move a bone. Example: Achilles tendon connects gastrocnemius to the calcaneus (heel bone).

Ligament

An elastic band of connective tissue that connects bone to bone. Provides stability to joints. Less vascular than tendons — slower to heal. Example: ACL (anterior cruciate ligament) connects femur to tibia.

Cartilage

Soft, gel-like padding between bones. Protects joints from friction and impact, facilitates movement. Types: hyaline cartilage (articular surfaces), fibrocartilage (intervertebral discs), elastic cartilage (ear). Avascular — relies on diffusion for nutrients, slow to heal.

🧠

Memory trick: Tendons = T for Two (Tendon → muscle To bone). Ligaments = L for Links (Links bone to bone). Cartilage = Cushion.

Local vs Global Muscles

Stabilisers vs movers — both matter in programming

Local Muscles (Stabilisers)

Deep muscles close to joints. Provide joint stability, not movement. Key for injury prevention and core function.


Examples: Rotator cuff · Rhomboids · Mid/lower trapezius · Transversus abdominis · Pelvic floor · Gluteus medius & minimus · External hip rotators · Vastus medialis

Global Muscles (Movers)

Large, superficial muscles attaching from pelvis to rib cage or limbs. Generate movement and transfer forces. These are the muscles clients primarily train.


Examples: Sternocleidomastoid · Upper trapezius · Pec major · Deltoid · Latissimus dorsi · Rectus abdominis · External obliques · Erector spinae · Gluteus maximus · Hamstrings · Rectus femoris · Gastrocnemius · Soleus

✅

Programming tip: A well-designed program trains BOTH. Global muscles move the weight; local muscles keep joints stable under load. Neglecting local stabilisers → injury risk.

Thermoregulation & Dehydration

How the body maintains core temperature and why hydration matters

📌

Normal core temperature: 37°C – 38°C. The CNS monitors temperature and triggers corrective actions when it shifts.

🥵 Too Hot — Cooling Mechanisms

🌊 Sweating: Sweat glands release moisture; evaporation cools the skin
🩸 Vasodilation: Blood vessels widen → more blood flow to skin → heat released via radiation

🥶 Too Cold — Warming Mechanisms

🔒 Vasoconstriction: Blood vessels narrow → less blood to skin → heat retained near core
🔥 Thermogenesis: Shivering muscles + hormone release → increased metabolism → more heat

💧

Dehydration: When the body loses more water than it takes in, thermoregulation is impaired. Sweating requires water — a dehydrated body cannot cool itself effectively. Water in blood also absorbs and moves heat to vital organs when conserving warmth. As a PT, ensure clients remain hydrated throughout exercise.

Forces, Levers & Sources of Resistance

The mechanics behind how the body moves and what it works against

4 Forces During Exercise

1. Gravitational Force

The downward pull of gravity on the body, proportional to mass. Acts on every movement — especially important in resistance training.

2. Ground-Reaction Force

Force exerted by the ground back on the body in contact with it. Equal and opposite to the gravitational force (Newton's 3rd Law).

3. Muscle Force

The force skeletal muscle pulls on bones during contraction — creates movement at joints.

4. Joint-Reaction Force

Force generated within a joint in response to all other forces acting on it. High joint-reaction forces can cause injury if not managed.

3 Classes of Levers

ClassOrderAdvantageBody Example
1st ClassEffort — Fulcrum — LoadDepends on position; rare in bodyTriceps extension (elbow joint = fulcrum between triceps and load)
2nd ClassFulcrum — Load — EffortMechanical advantage (power)Calf raise — toes = fulcrum, body weight = load, calf = effort
3rd ClassFulcrum — Effort — LoadMechanical disadvantage (speed & range)Bicep curl — elbow = fulcrum, bicep = effort between fulcrum and weight
💡

Most skeletal muscles in the body operate as 3rd class levers — trading mechanical disadvantage for greater speed and range of motion.

Why shorter limbs = strength advantage: In a 3rd class lever, the weight sits at the end of the limb (far from the joint). The longer the limb, the further the weight is from the joint — which means more torque to overcome, making the lift harder. Shorter limbs = weight closer to the joint = easier to lift the same load. This is why shorter-limbed people tend to excel at raw strength lifts like bench press or squat.

The trade-off: Longer limbs move through a greater arc — giving more speed and range of motion, useful in throwing, kicking, and striking sports.

Sources of Resistance

SourceDescription
GravityDownward pull on any object — foundation of free weight training
Weight Stack MachinesUse pulleys, cams, cables and gears to control direction and resistance pattern
Free WeightsBarbells, dumbbells, bodyweight — provide functional resistance through varied planes
InertiaResistance to changing state of motion — important in explosive and speed-based training

Monitoring & Adjusting Practice

🔁

Monitoring the responses to changes you make does not end the planning cycle — it creates the beginning of a new one. Observe → Listen → Record → Evaluate → Adjust → Repeat.

Monitoring MethodWhat It Tells You
Evaluating client performance changesWhether programming changes improved actual outcomes (strength, endurance, form)
Measuring specific outcomesQuantifiable data — HR, BP, body composition, rep max — shows objective change
Assessing progress toward goalsWhether the client is on track — identify what still needs adjustment
✅

Key principle: Once you have new knowledge, apply it to programming, then monitor whether it produces the desired results for clients. Making ongoing education part of your role allows you to continually optimise outcomes.
